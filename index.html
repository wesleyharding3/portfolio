Feature 01 — a live fashion-showroom business

Showroom Inventory

Tenant isolation the database enforces — not a WHERE clause every developer has to remember.

Next.js 15Supabase / Postgres RLSStripeTypeScriptVercel github.com/wesleyharding3/wtf →

A real showroom runs its day on this. Stylists, editors, and celebrities borrow sample garments; each piece carries a QR code; a phone scan checks it in or out and records who has it, why, and when it's due back. Every client brand is a sandboxed organization — and the naive version of that, a WHERE organization_id = ? on every query, breaks the first time someone forgets it or writes a raw query.

So isolation lives in Postgres itself. Every table carries an organization_id; 28 row-level-security policies enforce that an authenticated user only ever reads or writes their own org's rows — no matter which API path, ORM call, or raw query reaches the data. A superadmin bypasses it; everyone else is sealed in by the database.

What made it hard
i.
Isolation as a guarantee, not a habit

28 RLS policies plus security definer helper functions (with a locked search_path to avoid policy recursion), and anti-escalation triggers that block a user from changing their own role, org, or billing flags. The correctness is in the schema, where it can't be forgotten.

ii.
Money, verified server-side

Stripe Checkout for a one-time stylist onboarding fee with the card saved on file, fulfilled by a signature-verified webhook that recomputes the amount server-side and never trusts the client.

iii.
The loan, closed back to the press

A check-out's purpose (editorial, runway, celebrity/VIP) links the loan to the coverage it produced. A tier-weighted, time-decayed earned-media-value ranking turns press into a measurable, indexed ROI — never a client's raw dollars.

iv.
One id, physical and digital

A single tag_id (abstracted across qr / nfc / rfid) prints onto both the binder card and the hanger tag, so the rack and the record can't drift. In-house QR generation, RLS-scoped so a brand can only mint its own.

World news arrives in every language and format — RSS, sitemaps, scraped HTML, APIs, even Telegram channels. The hard problem isn't collecting it; it's reading the same story in Mandarin, Russian, and Arabic and recognizing it's one story. That takes script-aware tokenization, translation to a canonical form, named-entity resolution against Wikidata, and an embedding space where semantically-near coverage clusters even when it shares no words.

What made it hard
i.
Tokenizing what whitespace can't

Script-aware extraction across Latin, Cyrillic, Arabic, and CJK — Unicode-range language detection overriding mis-stamped feeds, with CJK / Thai / Khmer segmentation. A frequency-list prefilter drops ~45% of LLM calls before they happen.

ii.
One canonical name per entity

Claude Haiku normalizes non-English keywords to a canonical English form; a three-layer cascade resolves entities against a ~21k local gazetteer, then Wikidata, then the model. Steady state: about $1.80 a day.

iii.
Clustering with no shared words

A local CPU transformer embeds every article into a 384-dim pgvector at $0 per run; k-nearest-neighbor + union-find groups coverage into story threads, with a guard against runaway "Frankenstein" clusters.

iv.
Ranking that stays fresh and fair

Bayesian classification blended with exponential recency decay (24h half-life) and diversity cooldowns, so a few loud sources can't dominate the feed.
