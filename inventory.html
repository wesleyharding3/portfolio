inventory

Inventory dashboard & app for Wear the Future · 2025– · private client system

A QR code on every garment, so a real Los Angeles fashion house always knows where each piece is — who has it, why, and when it's due back. Stylists, editors, and celebrities borrow sample garments to photograph and wear; a staffer scans the tag with a phone to check a piece out or back in, and nothing gets lost.

It's the house's working system, used day to day by 5 team members, 30 designers, and 50 stylists and talents. The shows the whole collection at a glance — every piece, its status, who has it, what it's worth. When a borrowed piece shows up in a magazine or on a red carpet, the ties that coverage back to the exact loan — 246 placements so far, turned into a number a brand can actually see.

The system runs several brands at once, and each only ever sees its own pieces — kept separate by the database itself, not by hoping every line of code remembers to ask. The same QR code prints on the binder page and the hanger tag, so and the records never drift apart.

How a scan works
  1. A phone scans the tag on the garment
  2. The system finds the exact piece
  3. Out or back — who has it, why, and the due date
  4. The event is logged to a permanent history
  5. The status updates for everyone, immediately
What made it hard
Privacy
Several brands share one system; the isolation rule lives in the database, so a mistake in the code can never leak one client's inventory to another.
Payments
Stylists pay a fee to reserve pieces — confirmed on the server, where the amount can't be tampered with from a browser.
Proof
Press coverage links back to the loan that produced it, turning PR into measurable return.
Two worlds
One code joins the physical garment to its digital record — the binder, the hanger, and the database agree.
Under the hood
Stack

Isolation
28 row-level-security policies enforced in Postgres, with anti-escalation triggers; correctness lives in the schema, where it can't be forgotten.
Payments

Earned media
